Policy Iteration → Policy Gradient: A Natural Evolution

Published: 19 May, 2025 |  at  09:30 AM
| Suggest Changes

As reinforcement learning evolved from tabular environments to high-dimensional, continuous domains, classical methods like Policy Iteration faced major limitations. This naturally led to the emergence of Policy Gradient methods — a continuous, differentiable, and scalable generalization of the policy improvement idea.

🔹 Classical Policy Iteration

Policy Iteration is a two-step process:

  1. Policy Evaluation:

    Estimate the value function:

    Vπ(s)=Eπ[∑t=0∞γtr(st,at)∣s0=s]V^{\pi}(s) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r(s_t, a_t) \mid s_0 = s \right] Vπ(s)=Eπ​[t=0∑∞​γtr(st​,at​)∣s0​=s]

  2. Policy Improvement:

    Update policy greedily:

    π′(s)=arg⁡max⁡aQπ(s,a)\pi'(s) = \arg\max_a Q^{\pi}(s, a) π′(s)=argamax​Qπ(s,a)

Repeat these steps until convergence.

✅ This works well in discrete environments like Gridworld, Blackjack, or small MDPs.

❌ But it fails in continuous action spaces, where arg⁡max⁡aQ(s,a)\arg\max_a Q(s,a)argmaxa​Q(s,a) is intractable.

🔸 Why It Breaks in Continuous Spaces

  • The action space A⊆Rn\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^nA⊆Rn may be infinite or high-dimensional.
  • arg⁡max⁡aQ(s,a)\arg\max_a Q(s,a)argmaxa​Q(s,a) becomes a non-trivial optimization problem.
  • Greedy policy improvement becomes either inexact, expensive, or undefined.

This is where Policy Gradient comes in.

🧠 The Natural Evolution: Policy Gradient

Instead of constructing a new greedy policy explicitly, we learn a parameterized policy πθ(a∣s)\pi_\theta(a \mid s)πθ​(a∣s) and optimize its parameters θ\thetaθ via gradient ascent:

θ←θ+α∇θJ(θ)\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha \nabla_\theta J(\theta) θ←θ+α∇θ​J(θ)

Where the objective is the expected return:

J(θ)=Eτ∼πθ[R(τ)]=E[∑t=0∞γtr(st,at)]J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}[R(\tau)] = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r(s_t, a_t)\right] J(θ)=Eτ∼πθ​​[R(τ)]=E[t=0∑∞​γtr(st​,at​)]

🔍 Policy Gradient Theorem

The core result from [Sutton et al., 2000]:

∇θJ(θ)=Es∼dπ,a∼π[∇θlog⁡πθ(a∣s)Qπ(s,a)]\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^{\pi}, a \sim \pi}\left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s) Q^{\pi}(s, a) \right] ∇θ​J(θ)=Es∼dπ,a∼π​[∇θ​logπθ​(a∣s)Qπ(s,a)]

Where:

  • dπ(s)d^{\pi}(s)dπ(s): discounted state visitation distribution
  • Qπ(s,a)Q^{\pi}(s, a)Qπ(s,a): action-value under policy π\piπ

This avoids arg⁡max⁡\arg\maxargmax, and works in continuous, stochastic, and high-dimensional environments.

🔁 Connection to Policy Iteration

Policy gradient implicitly mirrors policy iteration:

Classic StepPolicy Gradient Equivalent
Policy EvaluationEstimate Qπ(s,a)Q^{\pi}(s,a)Qπ(s,a) or Aπ(s,a)A^{\pi}(s,a)Aπ(s,a) using critic or returns
Policy ImprovementUse gradient of ( \log \pi(as) \cdot A^{\pi}(s,a) ) to improve policy

Instead of computing arg⁡max⁡\arg\maxargmax, we move in a descent direction where actions with positive advantage become more likely:

∇θJ(θ)∝E[∇θlog⁡πθ(a∣s)Aπ(s,a)]\nabla_\theta J(\theta) \propto \mathbb{E}\left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a|s) A^{\pi}(s,a) \right] ∇θ​J(θ)∝E[∇θ​logπθ​(a∣s)Aπ(s,a)]

✅ Summary

Classical Policy Iteration breaks down when action spaces become continuous or large.

Policy Gradient provides a differentiable, local, and scalable solution — performing the same logical operation: favoring better actions, but using soft, gradient-based updates.

This is why modern RL shifted from table-based algorithms to neural, stochastic, differentiable pipelines.
