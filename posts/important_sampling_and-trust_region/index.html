Distribution Mismatch and Why Small Policy Updates Work

Published: 20 May, 2025 |  at  07:00 AM
| Suggest Changes

When optimizing policies in reinforcement learning using policy gradients, we often evaluate objectives using data from the current policy πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​, even though the objective involves the next policy πθ′\pi_{\theta'}πθ′​. This introduces a distribution mismatch — and yet, it works well in practice.

Goal:

  • Explain how we approximate policy improvement objectives
  • Show the role of importance sampling
  • Derive a theoretical bound on how much the mismatch can matter
  • Explore how KL divergence and clipping stabilize updates in modern algorithms

🔁 The Practical Approximation Trick

We aim to improve the policy by estimating the performance gap:

J(θ′)−J(θ)=Eτ∼pθ′[∑t=0∞γtAπθ(st,at)]J(\theta') - J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim p_{\theta'}} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t, a_t) \right] J(θ′)−J(θ)=Eτ∼pθ′​​[t=0∑∞​γtAπθ​(st​,at​)]

This expression tells us how much better the new policy πθ′\pi_{\theta'}πθ′​ performs compared to the current policy πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​, using the advantage function defined under the current policy.

However, computing this requires us to sample trajectories from πθ′\pi_{\theta'}πθ′​, which is not available yet. So we rewrite the expectation over pθ′(τ)p_{\theta'}(\tau)pθ′​(τ) using importance sampling:

Given two distributions ppp and qqq, and a function fff, we use the identity:

Ex∼p(x)[f(x)]=Ex∼q(x)[p(x)q(x)f(x)]\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p(x)}[f(x)] = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim q(x)} \left[ \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} f(x) \right] Ex∼p(x)​[f(x)]=Ex∼q(x)​[q(x)p(x)​f(x)]

Now break the trajectory distribution into per-step terms:

Eτ∼pθ′[∑tγtAπθ(st,at)]=∑tEst∼pθ′(st),at∼πθ′(at∣st)[γtAπθ(st,at)]\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim p_{\theta'}} \left[ \sum_t \gamma^t A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t, a_t) \right] = \sum_t \mathbb{E}_{s_t \sim p_{\theta'}(s_t), a_t \sim \pi_{\theta'}(a_t | s_t)} \left[ \gamma^t A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t, a_t) \right] Eτ∼pθ′​​[t∑​γtAπθ​(st​,at​)]=t∑​Est​∼pθ′​(st​),at​∼πθ′​(at​∣st​)​[γtAπθ​(st​,at​)]

Using importance sampling with respect to πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​:

=∑tEst∼pθ′(st),at∼πθ(at∣st)[πθ′(at∣st)πθ(at∣st)γtAπθ(st,at)]= \sum_t \mathbb{E}_{s_t \sim p_{\theta'}(s_t), a_t \sim \pi_\theta(a_t | s_t)} \left[ \frac{\pi_{\theta'}(a_t | s_t)}{\pi_\theta(a_t | s_t)} \gamma^t A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t, a_t) \right] =t∑​Est​∼pθ′​(st​),at​∼πθ​(at​∣st​)​[πθ​(at​∣st​)πθ′​(at​∣st​)​γtAπθ​(st​,at​)]

To simplify computation further, we approximate the state distribution pθ′(st)≈pθ(st)p_{\theta'}(s_t) \approx p_\theta(s_t)pθ′​(st​)≈pθ​(st​), yielding:

Aˉ(θ′):=∑tEst∼pθ(st),at∼πθ(at∣st)[πθ′(at∣st)πθ(at∣st)γtAπθ(st,at)]\bar{A}(\theta') := \sum_t \mathbb{E}_{s_t \sim p_\theta(s_t), a_t \sim \pi_\theta(a_t | s_t)} \left[ \frac{\pi_{\theta'}(a_t | s_t)}{\pi_\theta(a_t | s_t)} \gamma^t A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t, a_t) \right] Aˉ(θ′):=t∑​Est​∼pθ​(st​),at​∼πθ​(at​∣st​)​[πθ​(at​∣st​)πθ′​(at​∣st​)​γtAπθ​(st​,at​)]

This approximation allows the optimization:

θ′←arg⁡max⁡θ′Aˉ(θ′)\theta' \leftarrow \arg\max_{\theta'} \bar{A}(\theta') θ′←argθ′max​Aˉ(θ′)

This is the objective used in algorithms like TRPO, PPO, and REINFORCE with importance sampling.

⚠️ The Approximation Assumption

We replace:

Est∼pθ′(st)→Est∼pθ(st)\mathbb{E}_{s_t \sim p_{\theta'}(s_t)} \rightarrow \mathbb{E}_{s_t \sim p_\theta(s_t)} Est​∼pθ′​(st​)​→Est​∼pθ​(st​)​

This is not strictly correct — it introduces bias. But it’s justified when πθ′≈πθ\pi_{\theta'} \approx \pi_\thetaπθ′​≈πθ​.

In practice:

  • TRPO enforces DKL(πθ∥πθ′)≤δD_{\text{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\theta'}) \leq \deltaDKL​(πθ​∥πθ′​)≤δ
  • PPO uses clipping: rt=πθ′(a∣s)πθ(a∣s)r_t = \frac{\pi_{\theta'}(a|s)}{\pi_\theta(a|s)}rt​=πθ​(a∣s)πθ′​(a∣s)​, bounded in [1−ϵ,1+ϵ][1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon][1−ϵ,1+ϵ]

✅ Theoretical Justification: Bounding the Distribution Drift

We now prove that the state visitation distributions pθ(st)p_{\theta}(s_t)pθ​(st​) and pθ′(st)p_{\theta'}(s_t)pθ′​(st​) are close when policies are close.

🔹 Setup:

  • Assume πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​ is a deterministic policy: at=πθ(st)a_t = \pi_\theta(s_t)at​=πθ​(st​)
  • Let ϵ\epsilonϵ be the probability that πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​ and πθ′\pi_{\theta'}πθ′​ choose different actions at a state:
Pr⁡[πθ(s)≠πθ′(s)]≤ϵ\Pr[\pi_\theta(s) \neq \pi_{\theta'}(s)] \leq \epsilon Pr[πθ​(s)=πθ′​(s)]≤ϵ

🔸 Claim:

The distribution over states at time ttt under the new policy can be written as:

pθ′(st)=(1−ϵ)tpθ(st)+(1−(1−ϵ)t)pmistake(st)p_{\theta'}(s_t) = (1 - \epsilon)^t p_\theta(s_t) + \left(1 - (1 - \epsilon)^t\right) p_{\text{mistake}}(s_t) pθ′​(st​)=(1−ϵ)tpθ​(st​)+(1−(1−ϵ)t)pmistake​(st​)

Here:

  • (1−ϵ)t(1 - \epsilon)^t(1−ϵ)t: probability we made no mistakes over t steps
  • pmistakep_{\text{mistake}}pmistake​: some unknown distribution due to diverging behavior

🔹 Bounding the Distance

Use triangle inequality:

∣pθ′(st)−pθ(st)∣≤2(1−(1−ϵ)t)|p_{\theta'}(s_t) - p_\theta(s_t)| \leq 2 \left(1 - (1 - \epsilon)^t\right) ∣pθ′​(st​)−pθ​(st​)∣≤2(1−(1−ϵ)t)

Apply the identity:

1−(1−ϵ)t≤ϵt1 - (1 - \epsilon)^t \leq \epsilon t 1−(1−ϵ)t≤ϵt

Final bound:

∣pθ′(st)−pθ(st)∣≤2ϵt|p_{\theta'}(s_t) - p_\theta(s_t)| \leq 2 \epsilon t ∣pθ′​(st​)−pθ​(st​)∣≤2ϵt

📐 A More Convenient Bound Using KL Divergence

Rather than bounding per-step policy differences in terms of total variation (TV) distance, we can use KL divergence:

🔹 Pinsker’s Inequality

∣πθ′(a∣s)−πθ(a∣s)∣≤12DKL(πθ′(⋅∣s)∥πθ(⋅∣s))|\pi_{\theta'}(a|s) - \pi_\theta(a|s)| \leq \sqrt{\frac{1}{2} D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_{\theta'}(\cdot|s) \| \pi_\theta(\cdot|s))} ∣πθ′​(a∣s)−πθ​(a∣s)∣≤21​DKL​(πθ′​(⋅∣s)∥πθ​(⋅∣s))​

This gives a differentiable and tractable surrogate for measuring policy closeness.

KL divergence is defined as:

DKL(p(x)∥q(x))=Ex∼p[log⁡p(x)q(x)]D_{\text{KL}}(p(x) \| q(x)) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p} \left[ \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \right] DKL​(p(x)∥q(x))=Ex∼p​[logq(x)p(x)​]

Hence, small KL implies that pθ′(st)≈pθ(st)p_{\theta'}(s_t) \approx p_\theta(s_t)pθ′​(st​)≈pθ​(st​), and is used directly in:

  • TRPO: enforce KL trust region constraint
  • PPO: KL used implicitly via clipping surrogate

🔧 Clipping in PPO

Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) avoids the complexities of second-order optimization (like TRPO) by using a clipped objective to restrict the size of policy updates.

🔹 The Motivation

Directly maximizing the surrogate objective using importance sampling:

Et[rt(θ′)A^t],wherert(θ′)=πθ′(at∣st)πθ(at∣st)\mathbb{E}_t \left[ r_t(\theta') \hat{A}_t \right], \quad \text{where} \quad r_t(\theta') = \frac{\pi_{\theta'}(a_t|s_t)}{\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)} Et​[rt​(θ′)A^t​],wherert​(θ′)=πθ​(at​∣st​)πθ′​(at​∣st​)​

can lead to large updates when rt(θ′)r_t(\theta')rt​(θ′) deviates too far from 1.

To prevent this, PPO introduces the clipped surrogate objective:

🔸 The PPO Objective

LCLIP(θ′)=Et[min⁡(rt(θ′)A^t,clip(rt(θ′),1−ϵ,1+ϵ)A^t)]L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta') = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \min \left( r_t(\theta') \hat{A}_t, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta'), 1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon) \hat{A}_t \right) \right] LCLIP(θ′)=Et​[min(rt​(θ′)A^t​,clip(rt​(θ′),1−ϵ,1+ϵ)A^t​)]

This objective:

  • Encourages improvement when rt≈1r_t \approx 1rt​≈1
  • Suppresses updates that would push rtr_trt​ outside of [1−ϵ,1+ϵ][1 - \epsilon, 1 + \epsilon][1−ϵ,1+ϵ]

🔹 Why It Works

  • The min⁡\minmin operator ensures that the improvement is monotonic and avoids overshooting
  • Clipping creates a flat region in the loss around rt=1r_t = 1rt​=1, leading to more stable gradients
  • It implicitly controls KL divergence without computing it directly

🔹 Visual Intuition

  • When A^t>0\hat{A}_t > 0A^t​>0: we don’t want rt≫1r_t \gg 1rt​≫1 to overly favor a good action — clipping caps the advantage
  • When A^t<0\hat{A}_t < 0A^t​<0: we don’t want rt≪1r_t \ll 1rt​≪1 to overly punish — clipping limits how far you move away

In both cases, clipping avoids large policy changes — achieving the same stability goal as TRPO, but with simpler first-order methods.

🔹 Typical Values

  • ϵ∈[0.1,0.3]\epsilon \in [0.1, 0.3]ϵ∈[0.1,0.3] — common values in PPO implementations
  • Used with adaptive learning rate and early stopping by KL threshold to further stabilize updates

🔒 TRPO: Enforcing KL Constraints via Dual Gradient Descent

In contrast to PPO’s clipping method, Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO) enforces a hard constraint on the KL divergence between the new and old policy.

🔹 Objective and Constraint

We want to solve:

θ′←arg⁡max⁡θ′∑tEst,at[πθ′(at∣st)πθ(at∣st)γtAπθ(st,at)]\theta' \leftarrow \arg\max_{\theta'} \sum_t \mathbb{E}_{s_t, a_t} \left[ \frac{\pi_{\theta'}(a_t | s_t)}{\pi_\theta(a_t | s_t)} \gamma^t A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t, a_t) \right] θ′←argθ′max​t∑​Est​,at​​[πθ​(at​∣st​)πθ′​(at​∣st​)​γtAπθ​(st​,at​)]

Subject to:

DKL(πθ′(⋅∣st)∥πθ(⋅∣st))≤ϵD_{KL}\left( \pi_{\theta'}(\cdot|s_t) \| \pi_\theta(\cdot|s_t) \right) \leq \epsilon DKL​(πθ′​(⋅∣st​)∥πθ​(⋅∣st​))≤ϵ

This keeps the updated policy within a trust region.

🔸 Lagrangian Formulation

We form the Lagrangian:

L(θ′,λ)=∑tEst,at[πθ′(at∣st)πθ(at∣st)γtAπθ(st,at)]−λ(DKL(πθ′(⋅∣st)∥πθ(⋅∣st))−ϵ)\mathcal{L}(\theta', \lambda) = \sum_t \mathbb{E}_{s_t, a_t} \left[ \frac{\pi_{\theta'}(a_t | s_t)}{\pi_\theta(a_t | s_t)} \gamma^t A^{\pi_\theta}(s_t, a_t) \right] - \lambda \left( D_{KL}(\pi_{\theta'}(\cdot | s_t) \| \pi_\theta(\cdot | s_t)) - \epsilon \right) L(θ′,λ)=t∑​Est​,at​​[πθ​(at​∣st​)πθ′​(at​∣st​)​γtAπθ​(st​,at​)]−λ(DKL​(πθ′​(⋅∣st​)∥πθ​(⋅∣st​))−ϵ)

🔸 Dual Gradient Descent

We alternate updates:

  1. Maximize L(θ′,λ)\mathcal{L}(\theta', \lambda)L(θ′,λ) with respect to θ′\theta'θ′

    • Typically use conjugate gradient to approximate this step efficiently

  2. Update λ\lambdaλ using gradient ascent:

λ←λ+α(DKL−ϵ)\lambda \leftarrow \lambda + \alpha (D_{KL} - \epsilon) λ←λ+α(DKL​−ϵ)

This ensures:

  • λ\lambdaλ increases if KL constraint is violated (too large a step)
  • λ\lambdaλ decreases if the update is too conservative

This is an instance of primal-dual optimization.

🧠 Interpretation

If the new policy is close to the old one (small ϵ\epsilonϵ), and you’re not looking too far into the future (small ttt), then the mismatch in state distributions is provably small.

This justifies using pθp_\thetapθ​ in practice, and training via gradient ascent on approximated objectives like:

J(θ′)−J(θ)≈Aˉ(θ′)J(\theta') - J(\theta) \approx \bar{A}(\theta') J(θ′)−J(θ)≈Aˉ(θ′)

Or using surrogate losses like:

LCLIP(θ′)L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta') LCLIP(θ′)
